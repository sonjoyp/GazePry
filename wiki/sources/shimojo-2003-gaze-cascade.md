Shimojo, Simion, Shimojo & Scheier — *Gaze bias both reflects and influences
preference*, **Nature Neuroscience 6(12), 1317–1322, 2003**, doi 10.1038/nn1150.
The **gaze cascade effect**: gaze drifts toward the option a viewer will choose,
before they choose it. Adjacent to [[recognition-knowledge-leakage]] as the basis
of a *preference*-probing variant of the D7 oracle. *Not in plan §21 — cite
author-year (doc-local [M3] in [[d7-recognition-knowledge-direction]]).*

## Key facts

- Paired-face attractiveness decisions: gaze starts evenly distributed, then
  **gradually shifts toward the eventually chosen face** — the "cascade."
- The bias was **significantly weaker in a face-shape discrimination task**, so
  the effect is preference-specific rather than a generic looking bias. (This is
  the same "task demands matter" lesson [[nahari-2019-concealed-familiarity]]
  reports for familiarity.)
- Manipulating **gaze duration** — but not exposure duration alone — **biased**
  subsequent preference decisions, so gaze participates in forming preference,
  not just reporting it.
- Relevance to GazePry: this is one of the three effects
  [[van-der-cruyssen-2024-validation]] replicated on a **commodity webcam**, so a
  preference-probe variant of D7 has the same feasibility evidence as the
  recognition probe.
